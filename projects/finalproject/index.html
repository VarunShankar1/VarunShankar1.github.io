  1. Florida Panthers - 122 points
  2. Colorado Avalanche - 119 points

Building an NHL team is a meticulous task. In a league where even the best players are only on the ice for 20 minutes at a time and draft picks are often mere teenagers nowhere close to making an impact on the team that drafted them, the best rosters are built by multiple moves.

Some moves get teams their stars, the foundational building blocks upon which everything and everyone else is shaped. Others bring in supporting casts that slot in around the lineup and form the connective tissue of a team.

“Teams that win, win because they have star players and because they surrounded them with talent,” Dom Luszczyszyn, National NHL reporter for The Athletic, said in 2016 in a piece for The Hockey News. “While the top three guys are vital to success, most team cores contain a couple more players and each piece is important to long-term success.”

Teams have three main ways of acquiring players: they can draft them, sign them as free agents, or trade for them. There are exceptions — players can be claimed off waivers or signed as restricted free agents. There are also indirect acquisitions where a team trades for a draft pick and uses it on a player.

Most of the time, NHL teams play with four forward lines of three players each — one center flanked by two wings. They’ll also add three defensive pairings of two defenders and two goalies, a starter and a backup.

Where did the best teams get those 20 players from? How many were drafted, traded for, or signed? What were common threads between the best teams?

The top five teams at the end of this years’ regular season were the Florida Panthers, the Colorado Avalanche, the Carolina Hurricanes, the Toronto Maple Leafs, and the Minnesota Wild.

Across those five teams, 38 players were acquired via trade, 32 via the draft, and 30 via signings or waiver claims.

Among those teams, Colorado traded for the most players with 10. Carolina and Minnesota drafted the most players with seven each. Carolina and Toronto tied for the most players signed or claimed on waivers with seven each.

Most of the teams’ best players came via high draft picks. Three of the teams boast at least three players taken in the top five of the draft, including No. 1 overall picks Auston Mathews, Nathan Mackinnon, and Aaron Ekblad.

The two teams that didn’t feature multiple top-five draft picks, Carolina and Minnesota, still largely assembled their core in the draft.

The Hurricanes drafted Sebastian Aho at No. 35 in 2015, Andrei Svechnikov at No. 2 in 2018 and Jaccob Slavin at No. 120 in 2012.

The Wild drafted Kirill Kaprizov No. 135 in 2015 and Joel Eriksson Ek No. 20 in 2015. The major exception comes with Minnesota’s star defenseman, Jared Spurgeon, who was drafted by the Islanders but failed to sign a contract with them and signed as a free agent with the Wild in 2010.

That draft-heavy approach only applied to skaters. No team drafted its goalie, as each of the top-five team’s presumptive starter began their career somewhere else.

“[Goaltending is] actually one of the last things you really need to worry about for your teams’ core,” Luszczyszyn wrote. “You absolutely don’t want a guy that’ll lose games, but … he doesn’t have to be elite, just good enough not to blow it. Goaltending is obviously important, and it doesn’t hurt to have an elite guy, but you can win without one.”

Another key aspect for all these teams is how much they spend. In a league with a hard salary cap like the NHL, it’s imperative that teams spend as close to the upper limit as possible. All of the top-five teams finished with under $2 million dollars in cap space. Minnesota, the lowest-ranked squad of the five, was the only team with above $200,000 in cap space.
